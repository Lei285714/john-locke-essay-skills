# Anti-Patterns (What sinks a John Locke economics essay)

**Where these come from — read this first.** As in the History category, the Institute publishes
only placed work, so this list is *not* induced from observed failures. Each item is derived from
one of three sources, marked `[rules]`, `[contrast]`, or `[craft]`:
- `[rules]` — the marking criteria and submission rules state or strongly imply it loses.
- `[contrast]` — the placed essays conspicuously avoid it; the absence is the signal.
- `[craft]` — a well-established failure mode in economics writing and reasoning generally.

There is one extra, thin signal here that History lacked: Conte placed first and Shah third on the
*same* crime question, so where Shah does something Conte does not, that is a weak `[contrast]`
between a stronger and a weaker placed essay — not a failure analysis, but more than nothing. It
is flagged where used.

Because none of this is grounded in a real failed sample, treat the list as a vetting aid, not
gospel, and prune anything that doesn't hold up. Use it in Stage 5: scan the draft for each, and
where one is present, name it and fix it.

---

1. **Describe data instead of arguing.** Walking through statistics, episodes, or a literature in
   sequence with no thesis driving the selection. The question gets answered by reportage rather
   than by a claim defended with evidence. Fix: every paragraph must advance the thesis, not the
   dataset. `[craft]` `[contrast]` (no placed essay is a mere data tour).

2. **List studies or numbers without analysis.** Piling up citations, estimates, or named
   economists without tying each back to the claim, so the reader must infer the relevance. Fix:
   after each figure or finding, state what it does for the argument — the placed essays make every
   number re-prove the thesis. `[contrast]`.

3. **Answer a different, easier question.** Drifting from the exact question to a vaguer neighbour,
   or quietly broadening it. Fix: keep the precise wording of the chosen question in view and test
   each section against it. `[rules]` ("answer the question as precisely and directly as
   possible").

4. **Ignore or strawman the strongest counterargument.** Omitting the best opposing case, or
   rebutting only a weak version of it. Fix: state the opponent's case at full strength — the
   strongest pro-case, the most cited contrary estimate — then answer *that*. `[rules]` (the rules
   name this explicitly as a cause of failure).

5. **Correlation mistaken for causation, or mechanism hand-waved.** Reading a causal claim off
   co-movement, or asserting an effect with no transmission channel — or a channel that would not
   actually produce the effect claimed. Fix: state the mechanism, and where a causal claim carries
   the argument, say how it is identified (a natural experiment, a controlled estimate, a
   difference-in-differences or instrumental-variables study). `[craft]` and invariant #3.
   `[contrast]`: Shah reads a quadrupling of homicides directly off cocaine prevalence, where
   Duthie and Tham are careful to separate correlation from identified cause.

6. **Ignore opportunity cost and trade-offs.** Treating a benefit as free, or a cost as decisive,
   without the other side of the ledger. Fix: for every gain, name what is given up; for every
   cost, weigh it against the cost of avoiding it. `[craft]` and invariant #3. `[contrast]`: Conte's
   entire argument turns on opportunity cost — the productive use foregone when capital and labour
   go into crime or its prevention.

7. **Ignore general-equilibrium and second-order effects.** Stopping at the first-round
   partial-equilibrium effect when a second-round or economy-wide effect would change the verdict.
   Fix: trace the effect outward — to other markets, to the exchange rate, to the responses of
   other agents. `[craft]` and invariant #3. `[contrast]`: Tham follows money creation through to
   currency depreciation, imported inflation, and trade retaliation rather than stopping at the
   domestic price level.

8. **Conflate positive and normative, or evaluate with no stated criterion.** Smuggling a value
   judgement into a factual claim, or asserting that a policy is "better" or a level "efficient"
   without saying by what standard and whose welfare. Fix: separate what *is* from what *ought* to
   be done, and name the welfare or efficiency criterion behind any evaluation. `[craft]` `[rules]`
   (quality of argument) and invariant #2. `[contrast]`: Poulter and Duthie both mark the turn from
   positive analysis to the normative policy question explicitly.

9. **A model with no data, or data with no model.** A framework that never meets evidence, or a
   pile of numbers with no analytical structure to make sense of them. Fix: pair them — let the
   model generate a prediction that the data then tests. `[contrast]` `[craft]` and invariant #1.
   `[contrast]`: Shah's utility model is filled with admittedly illustrative numbers and places
   third; Conte's marginal-cost model is fed with real cost figures and places first. (A purely
   illustrative model is allowed only if labelled as such — invariant #1 — never passed off as
   evidence.)

10. **Padding, or running over the limit.** Throat-clearing openings, restating the question,
    filler transitions, sentences that exist to reach a word target. Placed essays are economical,
    and exceeding the word limit means rejection. Fix: cut anything that does not advance the
    argument; push data and derivations into word-limit-free figures and appendices; leave a buffer
    under the limit. `[contrast]` `[rules]` (word limit).

11. **Quote-stuffing.** Letting quotations carry the argument instead of the student's own
    reasoning, or quoting at length where a paraphrase with a citation would do. Economics rewards
    reporting a finding in your own words; long quotation is rarely warranted, and it is an
    originality and copyright hazard. Fix: quote only the rare phrase whose exact wording matters,
    keep it short, and make the analysis the student's own. `[contrast]` (placed essays quote very
    little) and invariant #4.

12. **Decorative math or theory.** Equations, jargon, or name-dropped models that signal
    sophistication without doing argumentative work. Fix: include a model or a citation only when
    it earns its place by generating or testing a claim — Poulter's dense theory all pulls weight,
    which is why density alone is not the problem. `[contrast]` `[craft]`. `[contrast]`: Shah's
    quasi-linear utility functions decorate more than they drive, by his own author's note.

13. **A thesis too safe to contest.** "It depends," "both have merit," endless hedging — a position
    no competent economist could disagree with. It cannot change a mind and reads as unoriginal.
    Fix: commit to a precise, contestable claim; qualify it without dissolving it. `[rules]` ("the
    best essays change the reader's mind") and Pattern 2.

14. **Inert definitional or "since the dawn of time" opening.** A textbook definition or grand
    generality dropped in as filler. Note the contrast: Conte *uses* the definition of social
    efficiency actively, turning it into the working condition that drives the whole essay — the
    anti-pattern is the inert version that goes nowhere. Fix: open by destabilizing (Pattern 3),
    and if you define a term, make the definition do argumentative work. `[contrast]` `[craft]`.
