# Winning Patterns (Distilled from John Locke Economics Prize essays)

These patterns were reverse-engineered from five placed essays (2019–2023). They are
**judgment moves, not templates**. Do not impose all of them mechanically; select the ones
that serve the student's thesis. The highest-leverage move is not a single pattern but a
pairing — Pattern 4 and Pattern 5 working together: reason through an explicit framework, and
confront it with well-identified evidence.

Source essays referenced below:
- **Duthie 2019 (Winner, and Grand Prize)** — "What does economics tell us about the benefits
  and costs of immigration? What policy should we adopt?"
- **Conte 2020 (Winner)** — "What is the socially efficient level of crime?"
- **Poulter 2020 (2nd)** — "What are the most important economic effects of forced
  redistribution? How should this inform government policy?"
- **Shah 2020 (3rd)** — same socially-efficient-level-of-crime question as Conte.
- **Tham 2023 (Winner)** — "A government funds its expenditure by taxing its population.
  Suppose instead it relied solely on newly created money. What are the advantages and
  disadvantages?"

**On the sample — read this before treating the patterns as law.** Five essays is very small,
smaller than the History set, and skewed. Three of the five are from 2020. Two answer the same
crime question (Conte and Shah), and the rest cluster on macro, public finance, and welfare —
money creation, redistribution, immigration — with no pure microeconomic theory, finance,
development, or econometrics-style essay in the set. Four of the five draw their data and policy
overwhelmingly from the UK and US. And every essay here *placed*, so we see what worked and never
what failed. There is one thin advantage over the History sample: because Conte placed first and
Shah third on the *same* question, we get a weak within-question gradient — and the contrast is
instructive (see Patterns 4 and 5, and the anti-patterns file). But it is one data point, not a
controlled experiment. Treat these as strong priors about what wins, not proven rules, and stay
alert to a question that rewards a different approach.

One more caution specific to economics. Placed essays here range widely in style: Duthie is a
synthesis of the empirical literature with almost no model of its own; Tham hangs everything on
one identity and a handful of cases; Poulter is dense with named theory; Conte is a tight
cost-benefit micro argument; Shah builds a model and then, by his own admission, fills it with
illustrative rather than real numbers. There is no house style. What they share is discipline:
in the strong essays, every model earns its place by generating a claim that evidence then tests,
and every number is tied to a source. That discipline, not any one architecture, is the signal.

## Pattern 1 — Interrogate the question before answering it

Before arguing, take the question apart: define its key terms precisely, expose its hidden
assumptions, and where warranted split a reading the obvious interpretation collapses. In
economics the load-bearing word is usually an *evaluative* one whose definition fixes the whole
argument.

Why it matters: it rewards originality and quality of argument, and it stops the essay from
answering a vaguer, easier question than the one set.

- **Conte 2020**: makes the evaluative term do all the work — "social efficiency" is defined as
  marginal social cost equal to marginal social benefit, refined into the condition that the
  marginal cost of prevention should equal the marginal social cost of crime. The entire essay
  is the working-out of that definition.
- **Tham 2023**: splits the question the obvious reading fuses — *solely* financing spending by
  money creation versus *partially* doing so. The split is the thesis: infeasible as a sole
  method, useful in part during downturns.
- **Duthie 2019**: exposes the assumption buried in the popular framing — that there is a fixed
  number of jobs and that immigrants are perfect substitutes for natives — and names it
  "Malthusian logic" to be dismantled.
- **Poulter 2020**: borrows Stiglitz to reject the equality-of-opportunity-versus-outcome split
  as a false dichotomy, refusing the question's implied opposition.

How to apply: in Stage 1, list the question's load-bearing words and the categories it takes for
granted. For each, ask: is the criterion contested (efficient *by what standard*? welfare *for
whom*?), does the question smuggle in an assumption (a fixed quantity, perfect substitutes,
ceteris paribus, a closed economy), and is there a sharper split the obvious reading misses
(short run versus long run, sole versus partial, average versus marginal)? Build the answer into
the thesis.

## Pattern 2 — Stake a narrow, contestable, non-obvious thesis

The thesis is a single defensible claim a competent economist could reasonably dispute — never a
vague gesture. Originality often comes from an **added qualification** the question did not
anticipate.

Why it matters: "the best essays are those which change the reader's mind." A thesis no one would
contest cannot.

- **Conte 2020**: the socially efficient level of crime is significantly above nil — counter to
  the intuition that the right amount of crime is zero.
- **Tham 2023**: sole reliance on money creation is infeasible, but partial financing has uses in
  cyclical downturns provided the central bank stays independent and money creation is legally
  constrained. The qualifier carries the originality.
- **Poulter 2020**: among the effects of redistribution, increasing equality is the most
  important — a clear priority claim, defended against the efficiency counter-case.
- **Duthie 2019**: immigration raises productivity, wages, and living standards in the long run,
  and policy should move toward market-based allocation of visas — contestable against the
  popular fear it answers.

Test before locking: Is it narrow enough to defend in under the word limit? Could an intelligent
reader hold the opposite? Is it more than the obvious answer? A "necessary but not sufficient," a
boundary condition, or a "yes in the short run, no in the long run" is often where the originality
sits.

## Pattern 3 — Open by destabilizing

No throat-clearing. The reliable economics openings are (a) a popular policy belief or political
claim erected to be dismantled, (b) a vivid or counterintuitive fact or statistic, or (c) a
canonical assumption set up to be complicated.

- **Duthie 2019** (the clearest of the set): stacks up Trump, Bill Shorten, and the British fear
  of the "Polish plumber," then turns on all three by exposing the fixed-jobs fallacy beneath
  them. The popular consensus is the foil.
- **Tham 2023**: leads with the images "printing money" conjures — Zimbabwe, Weimar — then spends
  the essay showing when that reflex is right and when it is not.
- **Poulter 2020**: opens on the pandemic — all in the same storm but not the same boat — with
  the statistic that low earners were several times likelier to lose work, making inequality feel
  urgent before any theory arrives.
- **Conte 2020**: opens more directly, stating the counterintuitive thesis itself; the
  destabilization is the claim that the efficient level of crime is well above zero.

## Pattern 4 — Build an explicit analytical framework and let it organize the essay

This is the signature economics move and the one with no clean History analogue. Set up a model —
it need only be a simple verbal or graphical one — and reason the thesis through it. The framework
usually dictates the architecture; let it.

Why it matters: it is what makes the piece economic analysis rather than informed opinion, and it
directly serves knowledge, understanding, and quality of argument.

The frameworks observed:
- **Marginal-cost-equals-marginal-benefit / cost-benefit ledger** — *Conte 2020* builds the whole
  essay on MSC = MSB and walks a ledger: social costs of crime, plus an opportunity-cost
  dimension, then prevention costs, then potential benefits.
- **An accounting identity confronted with cases** — *Tham 2023* hangs everything on Fisher's
  quantity-theory identity, derives that money growth raises prices at full employment, then tests
  the prediction against Zimbabwe and Weimar and against the exceptions (Japan, post-2008 bank
  reserves).
- **An externality / deadweight-loss model plus theories of causation** — *Shah 2020* frames crime
  as a negative production externality with marginal social cost above marginal private cost, then
  layers in sociological theories of *why* crime occurs.
- **A social-welfare-function setup with policy-instrument decomposition** — *Poulter 2020* derives
  an optimum from a Bergson-Samuelson welfare function, then analyses three instruments in turn
  (income, inheritance, and Tobin taxation).
- **A verbal theoretical framework over an empirical synthesis** — *Duthie 2019* carries no diagram
  but reasons through a selection model of migration and a task-specialization account of why
  natives and immigrants complement rather than substitute.

The architecture menu that follows from the framework: a cost-benefit ledger, a policy-instrument
decomposition, a comparative-cases design, an empirical-literature accumulation, or a
model-then-evidence sequence. In Stage 2, pick the framework and the shape together, justify the
choice, and name what the framework should *predict* so Stage 3 knows what evidence must test.

A caution drawn from the within-question gradient: a framework is not a decoration. Conte's
MSC = MSB model earns its keep because real cost figures are poured into it; Shah's utility model,
by his own author's note, is filled with illustrative numbers "not based on real-life
observation." Conte placed first, Shah third on the same question. Build the framework to be fed
with evidence, not to stand alone (see Pattern 5 and the anti-patterns file).

## Pattern 5 — Anchor every claim in data, and separate correlation from causation

Generalizations are immediately grounded in concrete evidence: named studies, point estimates,
elasticities, named policies and episodes, real data series. And — the economics-specific upgrade
— a causal claim is distinguished from a mere correlation, with the transmission mechanism stated
and, where it carries the argument, some sense of how the effect is identified.

Why it matters: it directly rewards knowledge and use of evidence, and it is the clearest line
between the strongest and the weakest essays in the set.

Anchoring, done well:
- **Conte 2020**: UK police spending, per-prisoner incarceration cost, conviction costs by offence,
  Singapore's per-capita policing, the East German Stasi as a case in the social cost of
  near-zero crime.
- **Tham 2023**: Zimbabwe's monthly inflation peak, the Weimar reparations episode, a chart of
  money-supply and inflation co-movement, and named estimates of the GDP effect of tax cuts.
- **Duthie 2019**: the estimate that removing labour-mobility barriers would roughly double world
  GDP, the OECD finding that immigrants' net fiscal contribution sits between zero and half a
  percent of GDP in most member states, remittances exceeding a fifth of GDP in nine countries.
- **Poulter 2020**: the top decile capturing the large majority of US income growth over two
  decades, the Gruber-Saez elasticity of taxable income, a US-versus-Norway growth comparison, the
  share of UK estates actually subject to inheritance tax.

Correlation versus causation — the distinguishing skill:
- **Duthie 2019** separates a short-run, identified harm (Borjas's skill-cell estimates of lower
  weekly earnings for less-educated natives) from a long-run aggregate gain, and even flags that
  the OECD figure rests on *static* accounting — he is explicit about method.
- **Tham 2023** shows the money-inflation correlation, then explicitly says the consequences are
  seen more clearly through historical cases and a stated mechanism, rather than resting on the
  scatter.
- The contrast: **Shah 2020** asserts that cocaine obsession *led to* a quadrupling of homicides,
  reading a strong causal claim off an association without identification — exactly the move the
  higher-placed essays avoid.

Rule: a claim without a number, a named study, or a stated mechanism is a draft note, not finished
prose. A claim that asserts cause from co-movement, with no mechanism and no identification, is a
liability.

## Pattern 6 — Steelman and absorb the strongest counterargument

Name the best opposing view, state it at full strength, often concede part of it, then show the
thesis survives or absorbs it. Concessions are specific, not token.

Why it matters: the rules state plainly that essays which ignore the strongest counterarguments
are less likely to succeed.

- **Conte 2020**: builds the opposing case that the efficient level is nil — crime as the primary
  source of inefficiency — at full strength, with figures, before answering it; later steelmans
  the prevention-cost objection via Friedman's point that overspending to prevent a small theft is
  itself inefficient.
- **Tham 2023**: gives the pro-money-creation side its best argument — abolishing taxes would lift
  disposable income, investment, and demand, with an estimate of the growth effect — before
  showing inflation and inequality defeat it as a sole policy.
- **Duthie 2019**: concedes the wage-depression case where it is real, citing skill-cell evidence
  of short-run harm to less-educated natives, then turns it with the long-run productivity gains.
- **Poulter 2020**: concedes Wolff's argument that bequests can *reduce* wealth inequality, and
  takes the dependency-culture objection seriously before showing it fails empirical scrutiny.

Place the strongest counterargument roughly two-thirds through, after the positive case is built
and before the conclusion.

## Pattern 7 — Engage economic theory and the contest between schools and models

This is what makes the piece an *economics* essay rather than a data-journalism op-ed. Engage how
economists have *theorized* the issue, not merely the numbers, and where there is a live dispute
between schools or models, take a side.

- **Poulter 2020**: the densest example — a Bergson-Samuelson welfare function, the Easterlin
  paradox, Keynesian consumption, the Meltzer-Richard and Downsian median-voter models, Rajan's
  inequality-credit nexus, Stiglitz against Kaldor on whether equity and efficiency trade off.
- **Tham 2023**: sets the quantity theory against the cases that complicate it (Japan's deflation,
  banks hoarding reserves after 2008), and invokes the Keynesian multiplier for the pro-case.
- **Duthie 2019**: reasons through Hicks and the Borjas-Roy selection model and the Peri-Sparber
  task-specialization account, locating itself in the labour-economics literature on whether
  immigrants substitute for or complement native workers.
- **Shah 2020**: brings in the Chicago School's social-disorganisation theory and Durkheim-Merton
  strain theory, and switches deliberately to a monetarist treatment of long-run aggregate supply.

In Stage 2 and 3, identify which debate the thesis enters and who sits on each side. The student
need not have read all of them, but the essay should be *aware* it is taking a side in a live
conversation.

## Pattern 8 — Conclude by refining, circling back, and opening a horizon

Not a flat summary. Restate the *refined* thesis with its qualifications intact, ideally return to
the opening, and gesture at an implication or an unresolved tension rather than claiming total
closure.

- **Tham 2023**: closes on the refined position — not feasible as the sole method, but a useful
  last-resort tool in downturns under strict institutional conditions.
- **Duthie 2019**: returns to the opening contrast between evidence and "knee-jerk reactions," and
  opens the horizon of the tens of trillions in gains a freer labour market could unlock.
- **Poulter 2020**: opens a forward horizon — automation and technological unemployment likely to
  raise public dependence on transfers — rather than declaring the matter settled.
- **Conte 2020**: ends on a dynamic note, arguing that as surveillance technology improves the
  prevention-cost trade-off weakens, so the efficient level of crime should fall over time.

## Voice and economy

- Confident first person where it sharpens accountability, used to own the claim rather than as
  decoration. Several essays use a short author's note in the first person to set scope.
- Economical prose. Under the word limit, every paragraph must earn its place; cut anything that
  does not advance the argument.
- Define jargon on first use. Technical terms are fine — these essays are full of them — but each
  is glossed where it first appears.
- **Offload data, derivations, and figures to clearly-labelled tables, charts, or an appendix.**
  Where the captured rules exclude such material from the word count, this is one of the strongest
  levers in the category: the prose stays lean while the evidence stays rich. The strong essays
  here all use figures.
- A short author's note declaring scope and definitions is permitted and word-limit-free. Use it
  to pre-empt objections: Poulter uses one to restrict the essay to redistribution within a single
  high-income jurisdiction and among citizens rather than firms; Shah uses one to state plainly
  that his econometric model is illustrative, not built on real observation — a move that doubles
  as honesty about a stylized model (see invariant #1).
