# Anti-Patterns (What sinks a John Locke history essay)

**Where these come from — read this first.** We have no genuine losing essays to study; the
Institute publishes only placed work. So this list is *not* induced from observed failures. Each
item is derived from one of three sources, marked `[rules]`, `[contrast]`, or `[craft]`:
- `[rules]` — the marking criteria and submission rules state or strongly imply it loses.
- `[contrast]` — the placed essays conspicuously avoid it; the absence is the signal.
- `[craft]` — a well-established failure mode in history-essay writing generally.

Because none of these is grounded in a real failed sample, treat the list as a vetting aid, not
gospel, and prune anything that doesn't hold up. Use it in Stage 5: scan the draft for each, and
where one is present, name it and fix it.

---

1. **Narrate instead of argue.** Recounting what happened in sequence with no thesis driving the
   selection. The question is answered by storytelling rather than by a claim defended with
   evidence. Fix: every paragraph must advance the thesis, not the timeline. `[rules]` (quality
   of argument) `[contrast]` (no winner merely narrates).

2. **List without analysis.** Piling up examples or citations without tying each back to the
   claim. This is the failure that *looks* like Cho's accumulation but isn't: in Cho every case
   is explicitly made to re-prove the thesis. A list where the reader must infer the relevance is
   dead weight. Fix: after each piece of evidence, state what it does for the argument. `[contrast]`

3. **Answer a different, easier question.** Drifting from the exact question to a vague neighbour,
   or quietly broadening it. Fix: keep the precise wording of the chosen question in view and test
   each section against it. `[rules]` ("answer the question as precisely and directly as possible").

4. **Ignore or strawman the strongest counterargument.** Omitting the best opposing case, or
   rebutting only a weak version of it. Fix: state the opponent's case at full strength, then
   answer *that*. `[rules]` (the rules name this explicitly as a cause of failure).

5. **Anachronistic moralising.** Judging historical actors by present standards without
   contextualising how and why they acted — the exact error Shokar's essay anatomises. Fix: enter
   the period's own frame before evaluating it. `[craft]` (and invariant #2).

6. **Single-cause determinism.** Reducing a complex outcome to one tidy cause and ignoring
   contingency and competing explanations. Fix: acknowledge the other causes and why yours is
   weighted as it is. `[craft]` (and invariant #3).

7. **Padding, or running over the limit.** Throat-clearing openings, restating the question,
   filler transitions, sentences that exist to reach a word target. Winners are economical, and
   exceeding 2000 words means rejection. Fix: cut anything that does not advance the argument;
   leave a buffer under the limit. `[contrast]` `[rules]` (word limit).

8. **Quote-stuffing.** Letting quotations carry the argument instead of the student's own
   reasoning, or quoting at length where a short clip or paraphrase would do. Also an originality
   and copyright hazard. Fix: quote only where exact wording matters, keep it short, and make the
   analysis the student's own. `[contrast]` (winners quote sparingly) and invariant #4.

9. **Current-affairs op-ed with no history in it.** Opining on a present-day issue while losing
   the historical and historiographical dimension. Pattern 7 is the antidote. Fix: ground the
   argument in historical cases and engage how scholars have interpreted them. `[contrast]`
   `[craft]`.

10. **A thesis too safe to contest.** "It depends," "both sides have merit," endless hedging — a
    position no intelligent reader could disagree with. It cannot change a mind and reads as
    unoriginal. Fix: commit to a precise, falsifiable claim; qualify it without dissolving it.
    `[rules]` ("the best essays change the reader's mind") and Pattern 2.

11. **Decorative scholarship.** Name-dropping thinkers without actually using their arguments, so
    the citations signal erudition rather than do work. Fix: cite a source only when its argument
    or evidence earns its place in yours; Shokar's citations all pull weight. `[contrast]` `[craft]`.

12. **Inert dictionary or "since the dawn of time" opening.** A definition or grand generality
    dropped in as filler. Note the contrast: Lin *uses* a dictionary actively to extract a working
    definition that drives the thesis — the anti-pattern is the inert version that goes nowhere.
    Fix: open by destabilising (Pattern 3), and if you define a term, make the definition do
    argumentative work. `[contrast]` `[craft]`.
