# Winning Patterns (Distilled from John Locke History Prize essays)

These patterns were reverse-engineered from seven placed essays (2019–2023). They are
**judgment moves, not templates**. Do not impose all of them mechanically; select the ones
that serve the student's thesis. The single highest-leverage move is Pattern 1.

Source essays referenced below:
- **Lin 2020 (Winner)** — "How is the modern world different... when and where?"
- **Cui 2020 (Joint 3rd)** — same modern-world question
- **Conway 2020 (2nd)** — "Strong state: prerequisite or obstacle to growth?"
- **Park 2020 (Joint 3rd)** — same strong-state question
- **Shokar 2021 (Winner)** — "Should we judge the past by today's standards?"
- **Ashmore 2019 (Winner)** — Kissinger on peace since 1945
- **Cho 2023 (Winner)** — "Which characteristics distinguish successful movements for social
  change from unsuccessful ones?"

**On the sample — read this before treating the patterns as law.** Seven essays is small, and
the sample is skewed: four of the seven cluster on just two questions, and all seven are
*placed* essays, so we see what worked and never what failed. These are therefore well-supported
*hypotheses about what wins*, not proven rules. One specific caution had been that Pattern 1's
prominence might be an artifact of questions whose key terms are unusually open to definition
(*modern*, *prerequisite*). The Cho 2023 essay is the partial test: its question is not
definitionally loaded in that way, yet it still wins by interrogating the question — challenging
the *success/failure binary itself* rather than a single word. So Pattern 1 appears to generalize
across question types, but the evidence is still thin. Use the patterns as a strong prior, not a
checklist, and stay alert to a question that rewards a different approach.

## Pattern 1 — Interrogate the question before answering it

The signature winner move. Before arguing, take the question apart: define its key terms
precisely, expose its hidden assumptions, and where warranted, **contest its framing**.
The question is treated as an object of analysis, not a fixed prompt to satisfy.

Why it matters: rewards *originality* and *quality of argument*, and is the clearest signal
of a first-class mind. A competent essay answers the question; a winning essay first shows the
question is more interesting (or more loaded) than it looks.

- **Shokar 2021**: "The first question makes assumptions which require elaboration." Questions
  whether universal "standards of today" even exist (argues standards vary by geography/class,
  not just time), then reframes *"should we judge"* into *"how should we judge"* — the entire
  essay turns on this pivot.
- **Park 2020**: opens by defining "prerequisite," explicitly rejecting the "sufficient
  condition" reading (which "would make the question overly tendentious") and establishing
  "necessary condition" instead. The whole argument is built on this distinction.
- **Conway 2020**: narrows the vague "strong state" into the precise "domineering state,"
  defined by three explicit facets, isolating an arguable target.
- **Lin 2020**: runs "modern" through the Oxford and Britannica definitions, seizes on
  "self-definition of a generation," and uses that to ground the subjectivity thesis.
- **Ashmore 2019**: splits Kissinger's single sentence into two separately testable claims;
  defines "peace" two ways in the author's note and declares scope.
- **Cho 2023**: the target of interrogation is not a single word but the question's *binary*.
  She asks outright "Can social movements even be classified as successful or unsuccessful?" and
  spends the essay dismantling the success/failure dichotomy. Shows the move works even when no
  term is obviously contestable — you contest the framing instead.

How to apply: in Stage 1, list the question's load-bearing words *and* the categories it takes
for granted. For each, ask: is the meaning contested? Does the question smuggle in an assumption
or a false binary? Is there a sharper way to read it that the obvious reading misses? Build the
reframing into the thesis.

## Pattern 2 — Stake a narrow, contestable, non-obvious thesis

The thesis is a single defensible claim someone could reasonably disagree with — never a vague
gesture. Originality often comes from an **added qualification** the question did not anticipate.

Why it matters: "The best essays are those which change the reader's mind." You cannot change a
mind with a thesis no one would contest.

- **Cui 2020**: "modern is media" — compressed to a slogan, then defends one specific mechanism
  (media → imagined community → modernity).
- **Lin 2020**: "there has never been and never will be a universal 'modern world'" — a strong,
  falsifiable negative claim.
- **Park 2020**: "a strong state is a *necessary but not sufficient* condition for growth" — the
  qualifier itself is the originality.
- **Ashmore 2019**: relocates the cause — not the "unending quest" but the external Cold-War
  battleground that frustrated peace.
- **Cho 2023**: rejects the question's premise entirely — movements "do not succeed, nor do they
  fail; they evolve continuously," oscillating between activity and dormancy. Note the originality
  move beyond mere negation: she does not just say the binary is wrong, she *proposes a
  replacement lens* the question never offered.

Test before locking: Is it narrow enough to defend in <2000 words? Could an intelligent reader
hold the opposite? Is it more than the obvious answer? If any answer is no, sharpen it.

## Pattern 3 — Open by destabilizing

No throat-clearing. Two reliable openings: (a) a vivid concrete hook that ambushes a default
assumption, or (b) a scholarly foil erected to be dismantled.

- **Lin 2020** (the strongest of the set): a "guess the country" riddle — lists modern-sounding
  traits (meritocracy, complex law code, global trade, religious pluralism), then reveals it is
  Tang–Song China. Detonates the reader's Western-centric default in paragraph one.
- **Cui 2020**: second-person immersion — "Transport yourself to the moment when you open your
  eyes" — plus the statistic that most people check their phones within minutes of waking.
- **Cho 2023**: a paradox hook — Modi in 2022 celebrating India as a "manufacturing hub," set
  directly against Gandhi, who read decolonisation as rejecting Western materialism and warned
  industrialism would "strip the world bare like locusts." The juxtaposition makes the
  success/failure question feel unstable before any argument is made.
- **Conway 2020**: invokes Fukuyama's "End of History," then immediately: "the following
  decades have not borne this out."
- **Park 2020**: grants the limited-government consensus its strongest evidence, then turns:
  "However, this laissez-faire line of thought overlooks..."
- **Shokar 2021**: a primary-source epigraph (Chateaubriand), historically situated, then
  pivoted to present relevance.

## Pattern 4 — Let structure serve the argument

There is no house style. The architecture is chosen so that the *shape* of the essay does
argumentative work.

- **Controlled comparison** — *Conway 2020*: Russia vs China across three periods
  (1978–91, 1990–2000, recent). The parallel-but-divergent design isolates one variable:
  political control retained vs relinquished.
- **Thematic causal chain** — *Park 2020*: political weakness → unpredictability/underinvestment
  → clientelism/waste; then strong-state stability; then a fork between democratic and
  authoritarian strong states.
- **Dialectical ladder** — *Shokar 2021*: successive scholars (Berlin → Butterfield → Carr →
  Fricker → Unger → Weber → Croce → Williams → Evans), each tested and refined, converging on a
  nuanced position.
- **Accumulation** — *Lin 2020*: independent cases across time and space (US self-image over
  decades; medieval Islam/China vs Europe; the Aztecs; Qianlong's 1793 letter), each
  re-instantiating the subjectivity thesis. *Cho 2023* combines two shapes: first a dialectical
  critique (demolish "policy change" as a success metric, then "social impact"), then a long
  accumulation (Occupy, Atenism, the White Lotus, the Arab Spring, Hellenism, Land Back) where
  each case re-proves that movements lie dormant rather than die. The cascade itself is the
  argument that success/failure is the wrong frame.

In Stage 2, pick the architecture that fits the thesis, then justify the choice to the student.

## Pattern 5 — Anchor every claim in specific evidence

Generalizations are immediately grounded in concrete instances: named people, exact dates,
numbers, named policies, primary sources. Primary sources are quoted directly only where the
exact wording carries weight.

Why it matters: directly rewards *knowledge and understanding* and *use of evidence*.

- **Conway 2020**: Zhu Rongji laid off ~40M SOE workers (1997–2003), cutting their number 37%;
  Khodorkovskii arrested 2003, jailed eight years; GDP figures throughout.
- **Park 2020**: the 1973 National Investment Fund Act; named conglomerates (Samsung, Hyundai,
  LG); the Gyeongbu Highway; POSCO.
- **Lin 2020**: al-Khwarizmi and algebra; Qianlong's 1793 letter quoted verbatim where the
  arrogance of the wording matters; 12.3M in forced labour; internet inaccessible to 89.3% of
  African households.
- **Shokar 2021**: Code Civil Article 213 (wives as effective property) as the concrete anchor
  for the anachronism argument.
- **Cho 2023**: the Emancipation Proclamation undone by the "black codes" (Novak: "a close
  approximation of the now forbidden master-slave relationship"); the 1968 split of 42% vs 58%
  of Black Americans on whether Civil Rights had succeeded; the Great Hymn to Aten. Breadth
  across eras and continents — but note her base leans more on journalism and reference sites
  (Foreign Policy, History.com, The Atlantic, Harvard Gazette) than the dense scholarship of
  Conway or Shokar. It still placed first, so range and aptness of evidence can substitute to a
  degree for archival depth — but a strong scholarly base is the safer target, and verify every
  such source (invariant #1).

Rule: a claim without a name, date, number, or source is a draft note, not finished prose.

## Pattern 6 — Steelman and absorb the strongest counterargument

Name the best opposing view, state it at full strength, often concede part of it, then show the
thesis survives or absorbs it. Concessions are specific, not token.

Why it matters: the rules state plainly that essays which ignore the strongest counterarguments
"are less likely to be successful."

- **Park 2020**: concedes Venezuela, the USSR, and Iraq are strong states that obstructed growth
  — then shows the "necessary not sufficient" framing *predicts exactly this*, turning the
  counterexample into support.
- **Conway 2020**: concedes laissez-faire USA/UK also grew, and that corruption hindered Chinese
  growth "to some extent."
- **Lin 2020**: engages the scholarly consensus that the Enlightenment began modernity, then
  exposes its Western-centrism.
- **Shokar 2021**: takes Croce (the past is unjudgeable) and Williams (relativism of distance)
  seriously before showing their costs.

The strongest counterargument should appear roughly two-thirds through, after the positive case
is built and before the conclusion.

## Pattern 7 — Join a historiographical conversation

This is what makes the piece a *history* essay rather than a current-affairs op-ed. Engage how
scholars have *interpreted* the issue, not merely what happened, and take a side in a live
historiographical dispute.

- **Shokar 2021**: the essay *is* meta-history — Carr, Butterfield, Evans, Berlin, Croce on
  whether and how historians may judge the past.
- **Cui 2020**: Benedict Anderson's *Imagined Communities* as the spine; Habermas; structuralism.
- **Conway / Park 2020**: Acemoglu & Robinson, Douglass North, Fukuyama — the institutionalist
  historiography of growth.
- **Ashmore 2019**: names the revisionist "long peace" school; Pinker; Harari.

In Stage 2/3, identify which scholarly debate the thesis enters and which historians sit on each
side. The student need not have read all of them, but the essay should be *aware* it is entering
a conversation.

---

## Pattern 8 — Conclude by refining, circling back, and opening a horizon

Not a flat summary. Restate the *refined* thesis (qualifications intact), ideally return to the
opening image, and gesture at an implication or unresolved tension rather than claiming total
closure.

- **Cui 2020**: returns to the morning-alarm image; opens a horizon — post-national imagined
  communities (the BTS army) that may claim stronger allegiance than the nation-state.
- **Shokar 2021**: turns the question on the reader (future historians will judge *us*), invoking
  the unforeseeability of the French Revolution's aftermath and of 1989.
- **Conway 2020**: ends on the Belt and Road Initiative as the live test of the thesis going
  forward.
- **Ashmore 2019**: ends on Brexit and Trump reopening the question, hedging the future honestly.
- **Cho 2023**: closes on the refined thesis (movements "endlessly transform, oscillating between
  activity and dormancy") and opens a long horizon — movements we now dismiss as unsuccessful
  "may end up forging our global landscapes hundreds of generations from now."

## Voice and economy

- Confident first person where it sharpens accountability ("I will argue," "I wish to
  investigate") — used by Conway and Park, not as decoration but to own the claim.
- Economical prose. Under 2000 words, every paragraph must earn its place; cut anything that does
  not advance the argument.
- Define jargon on first use (Shokar uses philosophical terms but always glosses them).
- Offload supporting data to clearly-labelled graphs/appendices where possible — these do **not**
  count toward the word limit (Conway and Park both do this).
- A short author's note declaring scope and definitions is permitted and word-limit-free; use it
  to pre-empt definitional objections (as Ashmore does with "peace").
